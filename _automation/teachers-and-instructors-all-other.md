* Teachers and instructors have been given a low probability of automation but I think this conflates the repetitive aspects of teaching—such as marking—with the less repetitive, such as engaging in dialogue and discussion. And the repetitive aspects are certainly being automated (which I imagine most teachers will embrace).

* Online course platform Udacity, for example, uses AI for grading. For its machine-learning curriculum, an AI assists a human grader in identifying what lines of code might be wrong, and continuously improves its grading based on feedback.[^1]As more teaching goes online, AI could disrupt the profession more dramatically, given that it can tailor lessons to individuals based on observing their behaviour closely, in a way no human could at scale.

[^1]:	[Google moonshot lab cofounder Sebastian Thrun talks flying cars, automated teaching, and an AI arms race with China](http://ift.tt/2jWV4zj)
