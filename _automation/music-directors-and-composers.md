* Music composition is a challenge that AI researchers are tackling due to its requirement for creativity—something much more challenging for computers than calculation, and still several breakthroughs away from human-level in many domains. There has been much progress in recent years.
* Researchers have used a type of recurrent neural network (RNN) called an “Anticipation-RNN” to generate melodies in the style of Bach.[^1]
* A similar research program, "deep artificial composer" (DAC), can produce complete original melodies after being trained on a database of songs and learning to predict likely note pitches and durations. In one study, for example, it was trained on 2,158 folk songs. (While I haven't read the paper, this sounds very much like an RNN similar to those for text generation, but here predicting notes rather than characters.)[^2]
* Researchers have created a music-specific generative adversarial network (GAN) called “MuseGAN” that can create four accompanying tracks for a song when given just a piano track.[^3]
* When paired with a physical presence, AI can also learn to conduct as well as compose. Maestro Andrea Colombini, for example, trained a robot called YuMi to conduct an orchestra, and then it did.[^4] (This may be one of those areas, though, where humans will  prefer human work, even if robots do it quantitatively better.)
* Disc jockeys also face automation. For  an extreme example, researchers created a table called “Classyfier”  that automatically chooses music for a situation based on picking up sounds of beverages being consumed around it; wine glasses clinking suggest a sophisticated party, while shot glasses suggest more of a night club vibe.[^5]
